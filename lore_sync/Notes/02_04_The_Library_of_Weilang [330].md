# The Descent
And so it was that the peoples of Linaria followed Zhiyu and Zhaoman and Rou away from the mountains and monstrosities. And Weilang watched and recorded all.

The people lived and learned with the gods and rejoiced in the skills and knowledge that were shared with them. Neither war nor disease struck the people as long as the gods were with them. But, in time, jealousy crept into the hearts of some. While the sounds of crafters and musicians filled the air, others hid in the shadows and plotted. One human generation passed and the peoples prospered and spread. Another passed and the distances between peoples became far enough that communication stopped between some.

It was into this fracture that Yuan Xiang lightly stepped, and Maethirion reveled, and Changguo fell. For in peace, the chances of glory are slim, yet when chaos and destruction come, glory rises. Yuan Xiang whispered forbidden knowledge into the ears of the jealous. Maethirion whispered new languages into the ears of the distant. And Changguo, Changguo fell to madness in the chaos that followed. For twenty score years, wars ravaged Linaria and, through it all, Weilang recorded everything with a furious passion.

# Weilang’s People
Not all was war and chaos, however. Small pockets of people had stayed near to the friendlier gods and were protected from discord. Most notable was a small group of people that followed Weilang, carrying her tomes as she filled them. Numbering less than two dozen people, they labored endlessly to follow the goddess with wagons and massive packs, carrying the tomes that she had written. Among them, a young boy who trailed Weilang everywhere and hung on her every word and movement. As the years passed and the collection of tomes grew, the young boy grew up and took on the role of leader of Weilang’s porters.

“Goddess, we carry your tomes everywhere, but there are so many. And, with time, there will be so many more” he said one evening.

Weilang looked away from her current tome, her hand continuing to write. “Indeed, that is the nature of time and knowledge. It is ever expanding.”

“My goddess, pardon my forwardness, but would it not make more sense if we built a storage place for them? A place that we can guard and expand as needed?”

Weilang smiled. “You have a point, my warden. What would you do?”

The young man looked up. “Goddess?”

Weilang released her pen, which continued to write, and waved at the ground. “What would you do to guard the knowledge I record? Where would you build this storage?”

The young man’s eyes widened as the pen continued to write, but he quickly looked around. They stood atop a large, broad bluff which sloped sharply to the sea in the southwest and gently rolled off into the distance in all other directions.

“I. I would build it here” he stammered. “I would make a hall of shelves on which to store your tomes.”

She nodded before taking up her pen again. “I will return with more tomes when you are done, my warden.” With that, she took her pen from the tome and tapped it on the ground. “Here is a head start. These plans should provide enough for what I have written already, but I suspect you will want to build more for the future.” Lines of fire spread from the pen, outlining a small complex in burnt grass.

A knowing smile graced her face as she bent over and placed a kiss on his head. Turning to the rest of the porters, she smiled. “My porters. My wardens. I will return in due time, when you have completed the construction of this building. Until then, safeguard the tomes you carry.” With that, she stepped off the bluff and floated across the sea to the west.

The goddess floated away and the porters all watched her until she was no longer visible. The young man watched for several minutes longer as everyone else turned away. “I will do this, my worship, my goddess” he whispered.

# The Library Complex
Over two decades, the porters of Weilang transformed the simple design into a soaring temple to knowledge under the watchful eye of the young man. Surrounding a central tower, the initial library building itself was a square two hundred and sixteen feet wide on the exterior. The library galleries were fifty-two feet wide and three stories tall. A garden filled the interior space and surrounded the fifty-two foot wide octagonal tower. The tower itself rose thirteen stories above the garden, ending with a thirty-nine foot wide room at the top, with a thirteen foot flagpole topping the building.

A ten-foot-thick brick wall wrapped around the complex, providing protection from the wilderness. Smaller barracks-style buildings filled the space between the wall and the library, providing shelter for the porters and visitors alike.

The tomes that they had brought with them filled the entry gallery of the library building. In the wings, additional tomes were stored, having been written by the porters themselves. By the time the flagpole was added to the central tower to complete the complex, less than ten percent of the shelves were filled.

Several months after hanging a flag of Weilang’s emblem on the flagpole, a man stood at the gate in the wall, looking out at the land sprawling before him. His robes marked him as the chief librarian. A young woman walked up behind him.

“Pardon my forwardness, Librarian Sinjin, but why do you stand here each day looking over the land?” she asked.

The man smiled and turned to the woman. “Librarian Belen, why so formal?” he replied. “Come now, Nashiri, we have worked together for a decade on this project, we should be past the use of titles. Yet, you always address me as ‘Librarian’.”

Nashiri blushed. “It does not feel … right. You are our leader. For many of us, you are the only leader we have known since Weilang journeyed across the sea. To use your given name would be disrespectful … unless …” she trailed off.

The man raised one eyebrow. “Unless what?” he prompted with a grin.

Nashiri’s blush deepened and she stammered. “U-u-unless, one were to become more familiar with you” she said rapidly, ducking her head to look at her feet.

The man smiled sadly. “I thank you for the offer, Nashiri. Truly, I am honored that you think of me like that. But my heart has already been given.” He gently touched her shoulder, looking past her. “Perhaps if we lived in another time. But do not worry, from the look on Scribe Darger’s face, I believe he wishes to … become more familiar with you.”

Nashiri glanced around and noticed the branches of short tree waving. She sighed. “Kenshi is persistent” she said. With a quick shake, she straightened herself and brushed the front of her robes. “It is true that he is a nice boy … man. But his puppy dog eyes are tiresome. Perhaps I should talk with him about that.” She glanced over her shoulder and nodded at the head librarian. “I thank you, Zhied, for your patience and wisdom.”

Zhied smiled and nodded. “Go. Your path leads that way. Mine is here, waiting.”

Nashiri bowed quickly and strode off toward the shaking branches.

A week later, as the sun was creeping over the horizon, Zhied threw open the gates to the complex and yelped in surprise. Before him stood Weilang, hand raised to knock on the gate. He quickly dropped to his knees and cried out “My goddess! You have returned!”

Weilang looked down at him and then at the gate, the walls, and the buildings. “It has been a long time, yes?” she asked.

Zhied nodded. “It has been twenty years and some, my goddess, since you left across the sea” he rushed.

She nodded. “I see. And, as all things do, you have grown up, my warden.” She reached down with her left hand and, using the rod she held, lifted Zhied’s face to look at her. “You have done well. Rise and show me what has been built” she said, smiling.

Zhied scrambled to his feet and self-consciously brushed the dirt from his robes. “Of course, my goddess. You have arrived at an auspicious time. Zhiyu’s blessings graced us not two months ago. The garden has been in bloom ever since.”

Weilang nodded as they walked into the complex. “The wall was needed because there are still beasts and monstrosities that roam the lands, but the gates are opened each morning to allow entrance to any that wish to join us. The outer ring is filled with the homes and shops of our people. We have many who spend their time making paper, books, and ink for those that record the stories of our visitors” Zhied explained as they walked around the low buildings.

# The Library
“Here is the main entrance of the library” he gestured as they approached the thirteen steps leading to the library doors. “There are smaller entrances on each of the other sides, but this is the only one that opens for visitors” he said as they walked up the steps. “It is also the entrance closest to the books” he grinned as Weilang raised an eyebrow.

Weilang relaxed. “I was about to ask why we would go to the visitor’s entrance” she said with a smile. Zhied placed his hands on the thick wooden handles and pulled gently. The doors swung open to reveal the visitor’s gallery.

The gallery was a perfect fifty foot circle stretching up the full three stories of the building. The second and third floors were ringed with ornately carved wooden railings. The center of the gallery was a circular desk in which three people sat. Zhied walked up to them with Weilang a step behind him.

“Librarians” he said, clearing his throat “Weilang has returned.” The three librarians shot to their feet and immediately bowed deeply.

“Goddess, welcome home” they said in unison.

Weilang took an involuntary step back and looked at Zhied. “Warden, we must talk. In private.”

The three librarians looked at each other in shock and dropped to their knees behind the desk. Zhied chuckled. “I figured we would, my goddess. This way” he said, gesturing to the far side of the gallery where open doors led out to the gardens.

# The Garden

They walked down the three steps from the door to a white gravel path that wound through the garden. Flowers of every size and variety were arranged around the outside of the garden, providing a buffer from the building and the elegantly groomed trees and hedges. The hedges formed intimate pockets around small seating areas, while benches wrapped around fruit trees of all kinds. Weilang walked alongside Zhied, her head twisting and turning to seeing everything. Zhied chuckled softly.

“My goddess, pardon my amusement. You have the same expression right now that novitiates have the first time they are shown the garden. It never occurred to me that the goddess of knowledge would share their wonder.”

Weilang looked sharply at Zhied. “There have been times that the unexpected, shall we say, catches me by surprise. I am not omniscient, else there would be little reason for me to travel the worlds” she said in mild rebuke. “I see the touch of my siblings here as well.”

Zhied sighed. “Yes. It was perhaps a year after you left on your journey, Zhiyu and Zhaoman visited us, inquiring about our small settlement. We explained the purpose of the compound and Zhaoman blessed us with fair weather, safety, and a surprising resistance to aging and disease. All of your porters appear to be able to choose how old they appear. And no one has suffered from disease or illness in your absence. We have been very fortunate in that regard.”

Weilang nodded, but her brow creased in concern.

“Zhiyu ensured we had bountiful harvests and long growing seasons so that we did not fear for lack of food and our craftsmen were blessed with unerring skills ensuring we did not waste any of our resources. When I explained to her the reason for the tower and the gardens, she aided me in creating a particular gift for you” he said with a smile.

He led the way through the gardens, slowly enough that Weilang could look around in wonder, but with a directness that was obvious despite the winding paths. Suddenly, he stopped and turned to face her. “My goddess, I beg of you to trust me and close your eyes” he said, head bowed out of respect. “I will guide you around this final corner so you may see the gift clearly.”

Weilang scrutinized him with otherworldly eyes. The silence stretched yet Zhied kept his head bowed and his eyes fixed on the white gravel. Neither insect nor bird nor rodent made a sound as the goddess examined her warden.

After several minutes, she nodded. “Very well, my warden. I trust you” she said with her hand extended.

Zhied lifted his head and looked up into Weilang’s eyes. “Thank you, my goddess, for your trust” he said as he took her hand in his. “You honor me with this.”

She closed her eyes and smiled softly as Zhied slowly backed up and guided her around the corner.  Finally, he had her positioned in the middle of the small cobble patio in front of the tower. “We are here. You may open your eyes, my goddess. Welcome to your sanctuary” he whispered reverently.

# The Tower Sanctuary

Weilang opened her eyes, her hand still gently clasping Zhied’s hand. They stood in front of the central tower, in a patch of sunlight. The steps were flanked by two fruit trees in full bloom. A riot of flowers and fruit filled the branches of each tree with almost every fruit. Apples, oranges, cherries, and pears were just some of the fruit that hung from the branches. The door was constructed of a golden wood that shone in the light and contrasted beautifully with the ruby red border around it. The white walls glowed in the light.

She gasped as she took in the sight. “But, how?” she whispered.

Zhied smiled. “As I said, Zhiyu aided me with the trees. She taught me the methods of joining different trees together so they grow from a common trunk, yet still produce their original fruit. And the tower is yours alone. The interior was the last part of the complex to be completed.”

He bowed and stepped to the side.

“I will wait here for you” he said. “Please, take your time in the tower.”

Weilang looked at him and then at the tower and back at him. “I am at a loss for words, my warden” she whispered as she walked towards the door.

She walked up to the doors and pulled them open.

The first floor was clearly split in half. The main entrance opened to a shelf and cabinet lined foyer, where visitors could leave their shoes and cloaks and traveling gear. It was empty, but the intent was clear. A staircase circled along the exterior wall to the second floor. A door in the center led to a storage room with low shelves filled with garden tools and a narrow staircase that led to a door in the ceiling, which in turn led to an exterior staircase that led to the third floor. She walked around, letting her fingers trail along the shelves and over the tools.

The second floor was a dining room with a large round table in the middle and thirteen place settings. A screened off staircase led to the third floor. Weilang smiled in amusement at the assumption that all the deities would be civil enough with each other to eat a meal together.

The third floor was split between a kitchen and a storage room. The storage room held casks, barrels, and sigil-covered cabinets that were cooler on the inside. A door in the floor opened to the narrow exterior staircase leading up from the first floor. An interior staircase led up to the fourth floor.

The fourth floor has a low shelf that circled the exterior of the room. Small, wheeled cabinets were arranged around the room and a movable screen surrounded a low table with seating pillows. The shelves were topped with flower filled vases and small potted trees. The cabinets had drawers filled with tools and supplies for trimming the trees and arranging flowers in the vases. As Weilang walked around and reached each window, she unlatched the shutters and opened them, letting out a soft gasp at the view of the garden from each of the six windows.

The fifth floor held waist-high shelves filled with books. Several large, pillowed chairs and footrests were arranged so they were in the best possible light from the windows. Weilang opened some of the books to find that they were about the plants in the garden, the construction of the complex, and the surrounding lands. One was dedicated to the festivals and traditions that had cropped up during the building of the complex. She smiled as she saw the name of the author, Librarian Zhied Sinjin.

The sixth floor was a more intimate dining room with a small table set for two in the middle and cabinets along the outside holding utensils, place settings, and various tablecloths and decorations, as well as two more chairs.

Unlike the upper and lower floors, which each had six windows, the seventh floor was wrapped in shutters that allowed it to be almost completely opened to the outside. A single desk and chair was in the center of the room, with cabinets on either side. Looking closer, Weilang noticed that they were set in a circular engraving in the floor which, when she gently pushed on a cabinet, allowed the entire setup to rotate and maximize the natural light on the desk.

The eighth floor was a slightly smaller duplicate of the fifth floor, with shelves of books written by her followers.

The ninth floor was, surprisingly, an armory. Racks of different sets of armor, all in her human size, and stands of weapons, predominantly her favored rods, were set around the room, each with a moveable screen to surround them. The tenth held wardrobes and dressers filled with everything from elegant robes to mundane pants and tunics.

The stairs passed through a ten-foot thick floor to the eleventh floor and emerged into a tiled room which held a tub with raptor talons next to one of the windows and a shallow pool in the center of the room. Two movable screens hid the staircases from the rest of the room.

The twelfth floor was draped in fabrics of all kinds, from silk to linen to wool to cotton. The windows were covered by multiple layers of curtains. The floor was covered with luxurious rugs while the ceiling was hung with airy fabrics. In the center of the room was a veiled area, with a wardrobe, two side tables, and a bed. The frame was a deep red wood with a swirling grain and a clear lacquer coating. A bowl of fresh water sat on each side table. 

A staircase was screened off that led up to the thirteenth floor, another open floor. Here, a golden telescope sat on a tripod along with a desk and a cabinet of writing and drawing supplies. The floor was inlaid with celestial markings indicating what was visible in each direction while the ceiling, as with all the ceilings, was of the polished and bare rafters.

Weilang walked through the tower in a daze, amazed at what her followers had built. How had they known that she longed for a sanctuary of her own? A place, even for a short while, to hide from the influx of knowledge from the world. She looked out from the top of the tower, observing the garden grounds, the library, and the buildings and land beyond. Far below, a solitary figure knelt in the gravel.

Without a thought, she blinked back a tear and vanished from the tower to appear next to the figure. “Why?” she demanded as she reached down to turn Zhied’s face towards her.

“Why what?” he asked. “Why did we build this? For you.”

“Yes,” she said as she knelt next to him. “And more. But first, why?”

“You are the goddess of knowledge. You are our goddess. You liberate meaning from the hidden places and bring clarity and wisdom out of the void. Such knowledge needs to be protected and shared, something that is difficult at best if we are traveling with you. Thus,” he waved his arm at the surroundings. “A place we can defend. A place we can expand. A place from which we can share what we have learned with any that come in peace.”

“But, the tower” she stammered. “That does none of those things. It was not part of the plan I set out.”

# The Confession

Zhied smiled softly. “No, my goddess. It was not. Instead, it is there to protect you. I felt it important to give you a place where you can receive visitors or hide from everything, as you see fit. You are more important than any one of us and deserve to be treated as such.”

“I do not deserve any of it. I simply record the world. I do nothing to it” she said, her voice harsh with restrained tears.

Zhied reached out to cup her face. “You see all of the world. The beautiful, the ugly, the good, the evil. And you do nothing to it, but it does much to you. We all saw it before you left on your journey. I added this tower for you, but I was not the only one to build it. Your siblings helped create the garden sanctuary. Your wardens, the Librarians, all helped build the tower for you. Because you deserve it in our eyes. A place where you do not need to be strong for us. A place where you can rest and rediscover the joy of knowledge again.”

Weilang looks at him, her face covered in tears. “You honor me too much, my warden” she sobbed softly.

“Forgive me for my presumption” Zhied said as he wrapped his arms around her and held her as she cried. They knelt together for several minutes before Weilang stopped crying and Zhied released her.

As they stood up, Zhied smiled. “I will hazard a guess that this is a welcome and needed gift.”

Weilang nodded. “It is very welcome and, I think, very much needed right now” she said as she bowed to him and then turned back to the tower entrance.

Zhied bowed in return. “I will make sure you are not disturbed, my goddess.”

She paused. “Zhied? Use my name.”

He froze. “My g-” he stammered.

She turned back towards him. “Of all the peoples, you deserve to speak casually with me. No titles. No honorifics.”

He started to bow again but caught himself. “Of course” he said, before he paused. “Of course, Weilang” he finished with a nod.

She smiled. “Thank you, my warden” she said. “Thank you, Zhied, for giving me this sanctuary. If you return tomorrow, I will recount my journeys.”

With that, she nodded once more and entered the tower, shutting the door behind her. Zhied stood there, stunned, for many minutes.

Over the next several months, Weilang worked with Zhied and the other Librarians to ensure that her tomes were understood, catalogued, and shelved properly. She also recounted her journeys while Scribes wrote her words down. Each morning, Zhied would call on her and they would breakfast together and each night, after a communal meal with all the Librarians, Zhied would have tea with her  on the sixth floor of the tower.

One year to the day after returning to the complex, as they finished evening tea, Weilang took Zhied’s hand. “Do not leave tonight, Zhied” she whispered. He blushed deeply and swallowed a rebuttal. She stood up and pulled his hand gently. He stood up next to her and she put her other hand on his cheek. She looked him in the eyes before she closed her own and leaned in to kiss him.

# The New Peoples

Two years after returning to the complex, a dozen travelers arrived at the gates of what had become known as Zhied’s Phis, or sanctuary.

Zhied opened the gates and bowed. “My gods and goddesses, please be welcome within this phis. If it pleases you, I can give you a tour of the grounds.”

The lead traveler, an elderly woman, stepped forward. “I care not about your tour, Librarian. I wish to see my daughter. Where is she?”

Zhied bowed to her. “Goddess, Mother of All, she is within the sanctuary tower at the center. I will bring you there now, if you wish.”

She looked him up and down and nodded. “Lead on, Librarian.”

Zhied turned and began walking towards the central tower. Ai Xiang fell into step next to him, a frown on her face. The other eleven travelers followed in their wake. They quickly reached the library doors, which were opened at their first knock. Cutting directly through the foyer, they opened the garden doors and began the walk through the maze of paths.

After a few moments, Ai Xiang grew exasperated. “You lead us in circles, Librarian” she snapped.

Zhied bowed. “This is the shortest path to the tower, Goddess. But it is not direct by design. This area to designed to protect and provide peace for your daughter.”

Ai Xiang glared at him before she clapped her hands together and they were transported instantly to the gravel patch in front of the tower. “Do not hide my daughter from me, mortal” she snapped. “Wait here.” She pointed at the ground and Zhied felt compelled to kneel in that spot.

Ai Xiang nodded and stomped up to the tower door. She knocked once. Twice. Thrice. “Weilang! Open this tower now!” she demanded. There was no answer.

The Mother of All attempted to open the door, but it held fast. She clapped her hands again and vanished, only to reappear on the gravel patch.

She turned to Zhied and the other deities. “What is the meaning of this?” she snapped. “Speak!”

Zhied looked at her. “Goddess, your daughter is in total control of the tower. None may counter her commands within it. It was created that way on purpose.” He shrugged.

“How is that possible? How can mortals create things to keep the gods out?”

Zhied glanced at Zhaoman and Zhiyu. “It was not just mortals that built this place, Mother of All” he said.

She glared at him and then at Zhaoman and Zhiyu. “So, my children plot behind my back?”

Zhaoman chuckled nervously and blushed. Zhiyu bent her head and blushed as well. “No, mother. There is no plotting here” said Zhaoman, his belly jiggling as he laughed nervously.

Zhiyu nodded. “Indeed, mother. My brother and I, we saw how the world and, more importantly, all the knowledge that she uncovered wore on our sister” she said. “The Librarian, in his prayers, wished for a place to allow her a modicum of peace and protection. A place to recover herself, free from the pains of seeing the world and being unable to do anything to right the wrongs that she observed.”

“We took pity on him” Zhaoman continued, “and blessed him and his people so that they could properly honor our sister. We helped create this place and laid the protections that she now uses to safeguard herself.”

“It is not against you, mother” Zhiyu interjected before Ai Xiang exploded. “I feel it is something different. It is not a pushing away, but a shielding of something…” Her eyes flew to Zhied.

“A child!?” Zhiyu gasped. “Weilang has given birth to a child of this man.”

All of the deities turned to him and, with a collective gasp, said “What?!”

Zhied flushed. “I have followed the directions of my goddess” he stammered, “in all things.”

Zhaoman laughed heartily. Fenfang’s high pitched squeal of delight echoed through the garden. Zhiyu covered her mouth and laughed silently. Yanching slapped Zhied on the back and chortled. Eu-fen hummed softly and Changguo laughed like the winds of a hurricane. Maethirion and Shi Chuan stood next to Ai Xiang and glared at Zhied, while Chao Sheng sat on the steps and howled with laughter. Rou was statuesque, his arms crossed, with a frown. The only one that did not react in any noticeable way was Yuan Xiang.

At that moment, a high-pitched cry echoed from the upper floor of the tower and Zhied found that he could move again. “Mother,” he said to Ai Xiang, “would you like to meet your granddaughter?”

She looked at him sharply as he offered his arm. His beatific smile disarmed her and she nodded. “Yes,” she said softly, taking his arm. “I wish to see my daughter and granddaughter.”

Zhied walked up to the door and placed his hand on it. It swung open to let him and Ai Xiang in and closed behind them.

They entered the bedroom to find Weilang in bed, a feathery-winged baby girl cradled in her arms. Nashiri stood nearby, heavily pregnant herself, bundling dirty sheets into a basket with two other midwives. She smiled at Zhied before bowing to Ai Xiang.

“Mother of All, we are honored by your presence” she said. “Your daughter and granddaughter are resting after a long labor. They are both healthy and strong.”

Ai Xiang nodded and looked Nashiri up and down. “You are due soon. And with a child of Maethirion’s” she muttered. “Interesting that my children find mortals this intriguing.”

Both Nashiri and Zhied looked at Ai Xiang in shock. “Maethirion’s child?” stammered Nashiri.

Ai Xiang nodded. “He was always the jealous type” she said. “I wish you well though. He is unlikely to be any help.”

Tears welled up in Nashiri’s eyes but, before they could fall, Zhied stepped up to her. “Have no fear, Nashiri. You and your child will be welcome here, no matter the parentage. We will all care for them as we do with all children here.”

She swallowed and nodded. “I must take these to be cleaned” she said hurriedly. “Come, Asiru, I need help with this. Saida can stay and watch over the child.”

Asiru, a dark skinned elf dressed in scribes’ robes, nodded and quietly followed Nashiri. Saida, a brunette elf also dressed in scribes’ robes, nodded and sat next to Weilang before taking out a piece of embroidery and continuing to work on it.

Ai Xiang watched them go and then walked over to the other side of the bed. She bent over the bed to look at the child. A small smile crept over her face. She looked at Saida. “May I have a chair, child?”

Saida nodded and started to get up, but Zhied waved her back and pulled a chair over to Ai Xiang. “Mother of All, please sit.” He placed the chair next to the bed and leaned over to look at the child as well.

“She is truly beautiful” he whispered.

Ai Xiang nodded. “What is the child’s name?”

Zhied shrugged. “We had not chosen one yet. We did not wish to set a name before we knew who the child would be.”

“Pick one when Weilang wakes. The child must know who they are” she said sternly.

“Yes, Mother of All” agreed Zhied.

“And you are correct, mortal son. She is beautiful.”

Zhied blinked and blushed.

It was less than an hour later when Weilang opened her eyes to see Ai Xiang and Zhied sitting at her bedside. She reached out to touch his arm. “Husband,” she whispered. “May I introduce you to Jeleni?”

Zhied looked at her and smiled. “Jeleni? Yes, a good name for her” he whispered back. “She is beautiful. Just like her mother.”

Weilang looked at her own mother. “Mother, it is a pleasure to see you. What do you think of your granddaughter?”

Ai Xiang’s stern face melted as Weilang looked upon her. “My daughter,” she whispered. “The love I see in this room brings me joy. Your daughter is perfect. As are you.”

Weilang smiled momentarily but then panic flashed across her face as Jeleni stretched her arms and wings and let out a shriek of displeasure.

Saida was there in a flash. “This is normal, goddess” she said, reassuringly. “She is merely hungry.” With deft hands, she repositioned Jeleni so that she could suckle at Weilang’s breast and the baby immediately quieted down and gurgled happily.

Three days later, another birth shattered the morning quiet. Unlike Jeleni, however, this boy child was rosy skinned and two small horns protruded from his head. Nashiri was close to death for two weeks afterwards and Asiru tended the baby as a surrogate while she recovered. The boy was named Tereus by Nashiri during one of her few lucid moments during those weeks. She recovered slowly and, after several months, Nashiri was able to care for Tereus herself. Maethirion had left two days earlier and never did visit while Nashiri lived.

Over the next three years, a half-dozen more demi-children were born within the phis. Some had feathery wings, some had leathery wings, some had horns, some had chitinous spurs, but all were celebrated by the community.

Three years after Jeleni’s birth, Weilang and Zhied welcomed a baby boy with jet black spiral horn stubs and velvet black wings, whom they named Sione. And two years later, they welcomed twins: a horned girl named Kelera and a feathered boy named Garen.

By the time that Jeleni and Tereus were ten, the phis was home to two dozen demi-children and three times as many full-blooded children aged ten or younger.

Across Linaria, demi-children were born into all peoples and two new races would come to be known as aasimar, the winged folk, and tieflings, the horned ones.

These children may have been born during a era of peace, but their own children would not be so lucky.
